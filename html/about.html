Birth

I was born in Westford, Ma on a cold winter night of 34.8 F mean temperature. It was a clear night or so the database says. I was born to a loving Mother and Father, with one over protective sister. I’ll be honest, growing up my father was the star of the show. As a construction worker, he was always fixing, building, destroying, rebuilding things around our house. This is where I picked up my love for problem solving.

Fast forward through some confusing and awkward years as a teenager, I graduated from Westford Academy (yes, it is a public school) and was accepted to Northeastern University Architecture program. I think as a young impressionable youth I may not have understood the difference between Architecture and Engineering. I loved math, I loved designing, but within a few years I realized how little math Architecture actually involved and how form greatly outweighed function. I left the program to pursue a degree in Environmental Science. It was here I was first, unknowingly, exposed to Data Science. We learned how to use MatLab, ArcGIS, and other Data Analytics programs. In 2009, Data Science was just becoming a professional buzzword, but I had no true understanding of what that meant.

Modal title

Northeastern has an co-op program that requires students to work for six months, twice, in their time as an undergraduate. My first internship was for Raytheon as part of an Environmental Engineering team. Again, I was brushed by the Data Science spirit, not fully understanding THIS was my calling. I was given a simple task (among many) to calculate the water usage of our small building from bathrooms. This data was to be used for a larger Environmental Initiative. I ran with this task, collecting information on every toilet and sink product, scrapping data to understand the average usage for employees in certain age brackets and sexes, then compared this to our own employee registry. After calculating the total usage, I continued on, interested in understanding how much water and money we could save if we switched to low-flow toilets, urinals, and sinks. I presented this data to my supervisor, who immediately saw the impact as well. I was to continue this task for the entire Northeast reason, getting sent out to all the facilities to essentially…count toilets. After completing all the analyses I was recommended by my boss to receive an award and present my project to some higher-ups. I remember presenting my information on low-flow toilets and potential millions of dollars in savings to these members as they watched three other recipients discuss their work on missiles, and high-tech radars. I thought I was completely outclassed. However, at the end they all spoke up and said something I’ll mostly never forget (since i’m paraphrasing here) “If you can be given a small project with toilets and continue to push it forward and create something powerful like this, your work ethic and enthusiasm knows know bounds.”
